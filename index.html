About JDM

What JDM means?

Japanese Domestic Market is a term used to describe vehicles produced by Japanese auto companies that are sold only in Japan. There are many different types of cars available, including V6s and V8s as well as 4-cylinder and 6-cylinder engines. The top speed on most JDM cars is around 190 km/h (111.8 mph). Some of these cars have automatic transmissions while others have manual transmissions; however, there are also some dual-clutch automated manual transmission models available. To import a vehicle from Japan, it costs around $3000–$5000 depending on the model you choose. Since JDM cars have smaller engines than those produced by foreign manufacturers, they can be difficult to drive at full power if one hasn't spent some time learning how to control them before purchase.

If you are young and your parents won't buy you a car, you should drive a Japanese car because they are less expensive. This way, if your car breaks down and needs parts, you can go to any garage that has JDM parts because they are cheaper than regular parts. Another reason is because if the car breaks down and you need a certain part, it will be easier to get this part from any garage since most garages have lots of JDM parts because they are much cheaper than regular ones. If you want to boost your car, JDM cars are the best option because you can find turbos and intakes easily. The problem is that some of these parts can get really expensive, so if you get pulled over for being too loud with your exhaust, the cop might ask you to pop your hood, and if he sees illegal parts on you, he might fine you or even impound your car.

Sleeper cars

A sleeper car is a car that looks really bad and destroyed, but the car probably has 300 horsepower or more. People make sleeper cars because when they go to races other people think that there car is not fast so they want to race them and they lose. For example you can have a 1985 Mazda RX7 with a 6.2L LS3 engine that puts out 430 horsepower and only cost $8000 stock from factory, so why go spend $70,000 on a Corvette when you can spend $8000 on the engine and $10,000 on the Mazda RX7?
